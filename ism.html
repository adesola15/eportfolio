What do the authors mean by the term ‘Industry 4.0’ - give two examples.

Industry 4.0 is the fourth industrial revolution, which involves integrating digital technologies like the Internet of Things (IoT), big data, cloud computing, robotics, and artificial intelligence into industrial processes. This revolution aims to create smart, interconnected systems that optimise manufacturing and business operations, leading to enhanced efficiency and more flexible production models (Ghobakhloo, 2020).

Give two real-world examples of risks that fit into the authors categories.

  1. Data Privacy and Security Risk: In a smart manufacturing setup where IoT devices are used to gather data from various sensors on the factory floor, there's a risk of unauthorised access or cyberattacks compromising sensitive production data. A breach in data security could lead to loss of intellectual property and production disruptions.
  2. Technical Risk: Adopting a new cloud computing infrastructure for business operations introduces technical risks. Issues such as system downtime, data migration challenges, or compatibility problems with existing software could happen during the transition. These technical risks could impact the integration and functionality of the new technology.

Find another journal article that either supports or contradicts the points made in the cited study.

The case study by Markus Burger, Melanie Kessler, and Julia Arlinghaus, "Aiming for Industry 4.0 Maturity? The risk of higher digitalization levels in buyer-supplier relationships," supports the findings of Kovaitė and Stankevičienė on the risks of digitalisation in Industry 4.0. Burger et al.,2021 explore the challenges of increased digitalisation in buyer-supplier relationships (BSRs), emphasising the resulting complexity of supply chains. They stress differences in digitalisation maturity among companies, posing risks to BSRs because of varying technological capabilities. The study focuses on understanding and managing risks within BSRs, saying that the risks involve both technological factors and human decision-making.

Human decision-making is still important despite technological advances, and this supports Kovaitė and Stankevičienė's points on practical implications and introducing RADi as adecision-making support tool. The case study talks about the relationship between digitalisation and supply chains, showing impact of Industry 4.0 technologies such as IoT, big data, and automation. Both articles talk about the goal of guiding enterprises in managing risks and maximising digitalisation opportunities.

Optional Activity – GDPR Case Studies

Read the Data Protection Commission (2020) Case Studies (see this week’s reading list). There are several case studies from 2014 – 2018 concerning GDPR related issues and breaches. Chose a case study (should be unique to each student) and answer the following questions:
  • What is the specific aspect of GDPR that your case study addresses?
  • How was it resolved?
  • If this was your organisation what steps would you take as an Information Security Manager to mitigate the issue?

My case study is 2007 Case Study 8: Failure to respond fully to an access request (Data Protection Commission, 2020).

This case study addresses the aspect of GDPR related to subject access requests (SARs), in particular the obligation of a data controller to provide the requested personal data within the given timeframe and to keep that data until the end of the access request. It shows the importance of transparent communication between the data controller and the data subject to make sure that the scope of the request is understood and fulfilled.

In this case, the Data Protection Commissioner (DPC) determined that the educational organisation had broken Section 4 of the Data Protection Acts 1988 and 2003. The resolution involved acknowledging that the complainant's access request covered a broader scope than thought by the educational organisation. The DPC said that the organisation should have preserved the CCTV footage for the requested date and gotten clarification from the complainant before deciding the scope of the request. As a result, the educational organisation failed to provide all the personal data within the statutory 40-day period.

To tackle similar challenges, Information Security Managers should make sure communication with individuals making access requests is as clear as possible. Encouraging clear and detailed discussions, they should look for clarification when necessary to understand the exact scope of each request. It's important to establish robust procedures for preserving data when receiving access requests, especially for information at risk of deletion based on the organisation's retention policy. This approach makes sure of adherence to the obligation of supplying requested information within the statutory timeframe (Rösch et al., 2019).

Additionally, it's important to guarantee the organisation maintains thorough documentation on how it handles access requests. Regularly providing training to staff responsible for responding to these requests promotes a consistent and compliant approach throughout the company (Hansen, 2022). Conducting reviews and updates of data retention policies helps them stick to legal requirements. Consideration should be given to extending retention periods for data relevant to potential access requests or legal disputes, strengthening GDPR compliance (Hansen, 2022).

Implementing automated systems or alerts that notify relevant personnel when receiving access requests can enhance efficiency, ensuring a timely response and compliance with regulatory obligations. Seeking legal guidance is essential to gain a clear understanding of GDPR requirements, especially concerning data preservation and communication with data subjects during access requests (Di Martino et al., 2022). Finally, establishing a continuous improvement feedback loop allows for learning from past access requests and investigations, refining processes and reducing the risk of contraventions.

Unit 5: Future Trends in Security and Risk Management

Collaborative Wiki Development: The Future of ISM

In the conclusion of his paper, Aven (2016) proposes: How can we display risk information without misrepresenting what we know and do not know? Read the paper and then and propose an answer, based on the research around future trends presented in this unit.

  • Improved Visualisation Techniques: Enhance the representation of risk information through the development and application of advanced visualisation methods. Use graphical tools, interactive dashboards, and visual aids to effectively communicate complex risk data. This could involve the use of heatmaps, scenario analysis, and dynamic models to illustrate uncertain concepts in a clear and user-friendly manner (Aven, 2016 and Cox, 2012).
  • Integration of Probability and Qualitative Approaches: Combine probabilistic methods with qualitative techniques to provide maangers with a more comprehensive understanding of uncertainties. Instead of just relying on probability distributions, also use qualitative descriptors that convey the strength and type of available knowledge. This approach makes sure there a balanced view of associated risks (Aven, 2016 and Borgonovo, 2007).
  • Transparent Assumptions and Justifications: Clearly outline and justify the assumptions that are made during risk assessments in a transparent way. Communicate the confidence level in these assumptions and acknowledge areas of uncertainty. This transparency makes sure that risk managers are aware of the limitations of the risk information and can consider these factors in their decisions (Aven, 2016 and Cox, 2012).
  • Interactive Decision Support Systems: Develop user-friendly decision support systems that allows users to look into different scenarios, adjust input, and see how changes would impact risk outcomes. This would mean a flexible understanding of the risk, letting decision makers interact with the information and look into different perspectives (Aven, 2016 and Wu and Blackhurst, 2009).
  • Stakeholder Engagement: Involve stakeholders in the risk assessment process and change communication strategies to fit different audiences. Consider the backgrounds, interests, and expertise levels of different stakeholder groups to make sure that risk information is accessible and meaningful to a wide range of people making decisions (Aven, 2016).

References:

Borgonovo, E., 2007. A new uncertainty importance measure. Reliability Engineering & System Safety, 92(6), pp.771-784.

Unit 6: The Great Debate - What Will be the Most Influential Trend in ISM in the Next 5 Years?

The Great Debate – The Future of SRM

Each team will choose a trend that they think will be the most influential in the next 5 years and will prepare a 10-minute presentation on that trend that summaries the key points and argues why they believe it will be the most influential in the next 5 years.

The most influential trend in risk assessment and management over the next five years is likely to be the shift towards dynamic risk assessment and management, particularly in response to emerging risks and uncertainties (Flage and Aven, 2015).

Dynamic risk assessment and management involves a proactive and adaptive strategy acknowledging the changing nature of risks over time. Compared to conventional static methods, this approach involves ongoing monitoring, real-time adaptability, and flexible response plans. It considers emerging risks, and addresses uncertainties and knowledge gaps. Decision-makers are pivotal, actively participating in continuous assessments and changing risk mitigation measures based on changing conditions (Cheimonidis and Rantos, 2023).

The method focuses on the importance of ongoing feedback loops, allowing organisations to navigate uncertainties effectively. Dynamic risk management is especially pertinent in industries or projects susceptible to changing conditions, offering a comprehensive framework for dealing with both known and unforeseen risks promptly and flexibly (Aven, 2016).

Emerging Risk Concept: The concept of emerging risk, especially in relation to black swan events, shows situations where background knowledge is weak. In these cases, it suggest that a new type of event could occur in the future with potentially severe consequences. Weak background knowledge makes it difficult to specify consequences and scenarios accurately, which shows the need for different approaches beyond just pure probabilistic methods (Flage and Aven, 2015).

Adaptive Risk Management Strategies: Dynamic risk assessment and management emphasises the importance of balancing different risk management strategies in an adaptive manner. This includes using cautionary strategies and an increased attention to signals and warnings. Understanding the dynamic nature of risks, this approach calls for flexibility in risk management strategies to effectively respond to evolving situations (Aven, 2016).

Development of Innovative Modeling and Analysis Methods: Lots of research and development are needed to go beyond traditional modeling and analysis methods. This is important for handling different types of systems, such as complex and interdependent critical infrastructures and security-related applications. The focus is on developing frameworks that integrate standard security approaches and effectively address uncertainty in risk assessments.

Ethical Considerations in Intergenerational Decision-Making: Ethical considerations play a significant role in decision-making situations. Questions are raised about available frameworks, perspectives, and duty of care to future generations. Adding ethical principles into risk assessments is going to be very important, especially when dealing with long-term consequences and uncertainties (King et al., 2018).

References:

Aven, T. (2016) Risk assessment and risk management: Review of recent advances on their foundation. European Journal of Operational Research 253(1): 1-13.

Cheimonidis, P. and Rantos, K., 2023. Dynamic Risk Assessment in Cybersecurity: A Systematic Literature Review. Future Internet, 15(10), p.324.

Cox Jr, L.A., 2012. Confronting deep uncertainties in risk analysis. Risk Analysis: An International Journal, 32(10), pp.1607-1629.
