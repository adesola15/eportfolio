Unit 1: History of Network Security, Vulnerabilities and Approaches

Collaborative Discussion 1: Digitalisation – What are the security implications of the digital economy? - Initial Post

Read Wei et al. (2019) and Spremic and Simunic (2018) and then post a message that reflects your thoughts and responses to the questions below. You should support your arguments with appropriate academic references.


What is a 'fully digital enterprise'?

A fully digital enterprise refers to an organisation extensively using digital technologies throughout its entire business operations, processes, and functions. This involves integrating technologies such as AI, IoT, cloud computing, and blockchain to boost efficiency, foster innovation, and enhance customer experiences. Wei et al. (2019) explains that a fully digital enterprise in the power and utilities sector means seamlessly adopting digital technologies for optimising energy generation, distribution, and customer services.

What are the cyber security challenges/concerns with a fully digital enterprise?

In a fully digital enterprise, cyber security challenges come from an expanded attack surface due to extensive digital integration. These challenges include sophisticated cyber threats, data breaches, insider threats, and the mean there must be intelligent and effective controls. Spremic and Simunic (2018) highlight the need to move beyond basic security measures to tackle advanced and emerging cyber threats, focusing on the importance of a comprehensive, organisation-wide approach to cyber security.

What are the cyber security challenges for a bricks and mortar SME aspiring to become a digital enterprise?

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) moving from traditional brick-and-mortar operations to digital enterprises encounter challenges such as a lack of expertise in managing cyber risks, insufficient technical skills, and the need for comprehensive education. Heikkilä et al. (2016) state that there is an ongoing cat-and-mouse game in cybersecurity for SMEs as the shift to a digital model introduces new vulnerabilities, which means there should be implementation of sophisticated controls. Spremic and Simunic (2018) suggest that companies in this transition phase may underestimate cyber threats or express overconfidence in their existing controls, indicating a potential gap in awareness and preparedness.

Chidukwani et al. (2022) call for a more comprehensive and quantitative research approach to address the key challenges faced by SMEs and provide balanced guidance for cyber threat detection, response, and recovery. Also, even though IoT has given rise to security concerns, Alotaibi (2019) says there is the potential that blockchain technology could address these limitations by enhancing end-to-end traceability and ensuring data privacy, so there is hope for SMEs that are in transition that they will be more supported when facing their challenges.

Do you agree with the views expressed, especially in light of the 'energy crisis' experienced worldwide in 2022?

Taking the worldwide energy crisis in 2022 into account, I think that a well-implemented digital transformation can be part of the solution by enhancing energy efficiency and reducing environmental impact, but there would have to be careful planning with consideration of the different challenges faced by SMEs from different sectors. There are also new potential problems to consider when switching to a fully digital enterprise such as power shortages and the cost of backup infrastructure.

Collaborative Discussion 2: The Pros and cons of logging – The impact of log4j - Initial Post

Read Berger (2021) and Ekelhart et al (2019) and then post your thoughts on the issues of logging for security analysis versus the issues of log-related exploits. You should support your arguments with appropriate academic references.


Both papers discuss important issues in security log analysis, and they focus on challenges associated with log-related exploits. Ekelhart et al. (2019) take a semantic approach to security log analysis, and they point out the limitations in the current automated methods that use regular expressions and statistical correlations. They suggest lifting raw log data, modeling context, and integrating events based on causal relations for a more comprehensive understanding of unstructured log messages. Meanwhile Berger's (2021) article provides a practical example of the Log4Shell vulnerability in the widely-used Java logging framework Log4j 2, which was discovered in December 2021.

Both authors emphasise the need for automation and scalability in security log analysis. Ekelhart et al. (2019) highlight the importance of automating processes to handle the increasing complexity and volumes of log data. The Log4Shell case shows the challenge of scalability in dealing with widespread vulnerabilities across various systems. Contextual analysis is also a common theme, with Ekelhart et al. (2019) expressing that is it important to link events from different sources, whilst Berger (2020) discusses the impact of the vulnerability in the context of affected systems.

Berger's also talks about timely patching as a crucial response to vulnerabilities, with companies needing efficient solutions for identifying and prioritising vulnerable systems. Everson et al. (2022) say that situational awareness and continuous monitoring of systems is vital to combat vulnerabilities and mitigate the effects of the next critical open-source library vulnerability.

The interconnectedness and dependencies amongst different platforms and services needs to be considered, and the Log4Shell case shows this (Berger, 2021). Advanced, automated, and context-aware log analysis is needed to address the complexity and scale of modern information systems. Schmidt et al., (2012) say that this will include prompt patching and continuous monitoring because security challenges are constantly evolving.

Ekelhart, A., Kiesling, E. and Kurniawan, K., 2019. Taming the logs - Vocabularies for semantic security analysis. Procedia Computer Science, 137, pp.109–119. doi:https://doi.org/10.1016/j.procs.2018.09.011.

Unit 6: The Great Debate: The Future of the Internet

Unit 6 Seminar - The Debate - The Future of the Internet

Earlier in the week, your tutor will have allocated you into one of the following four teams:
  1. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on content centric networking (CCN and/or NDN or COAST).
  2. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on peer-to-peer overlay-based networking (BitTorrent, TOR, Freenet, KAD).
  3. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on the MobilityFirst architecture.
  4. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on the adoption of IPv6 and the rollout of the associated security measures (DNSSEC, HTTP/3, IPsec, etc.)

Each team will make their opening arguments in support of their position. Each member should provide at least one argument for their position – arguments can be presented in the form of a slide deck.

Each team should argue against the other positions and defend why their point of view is correct. Each team member should pick at least one opposing point of view and refute it.

I am assigned to Team 3.

Arguments for: 3. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on the MobilityFirst architecture.

The MobilityFirst prioritises seamless mobility, providing users with uninterrupted connectivity as they move between networks. This is vital in today's dynamic world where users frequently switch between devices and locations. MobilityFirst enhances scalability, offering a reliable solution for the increasing number of connected devices. Furthermore, its focus on naming data rather than locations aligns well with the evolving needs of a decentralised and globally distributed internet (Raychaudhuri et al., 2012).

Arguments agaisnt: 1. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on content centric networking (CCN and/or NDN or COAST).

Whilst content-centric networking introduces innovative approaches to content retrieval, it falls short in prioritising seamless mobility, which is a critical aspect of the modern internet. MobilityFirst's architecture, designed with mobility at its core, provides a mobile solution in a time when users expect continuous connectivity regardless nof their location (Amadeo et al., 2016).

Arguments agaisnt: 2. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on peer-to-peer overlay-based networking (BitTorrent, TOR, Freenet, KAD).

Peer-to-peer networks rely on distributed nodes, but they may face challenges in providing consistent connectivity during mobility. MobilityFirst has dedicated a focus on mobility, surpasses the limitations of peer-to-peer overlays, offering a more comprehensive solution (Kumar et al., 2020).

Arguments agaisnt: 4. It is our belief that the future of the internet is based on the adoption of IPv6 and the rollout of the associated security measures (DNSSEC, HTTP/3, IPsec,etc.)

Whilst IPv6 adoption is necessary, relying solely on this transition will not address the emerging challenges of mobility and global connectivity. MobilityFirst not only supports IPv6 but goes beyond, ensuring a more holistic approach to internet architecture that caters to changing needs (Jianping et al., 2016).

Reference

Amadeo, M., Campolo, C., Quevedo, J., Corujo, D., Molinaro, A., Iera, A., Aguiar, R.L. and Vasilakos, A.V., 2016. Information-centric networking for the internet of things: challenges and opportunities. IEEE Network, 30(2), pp.92-100.

Jianping, W., Lili, L.I.U. and Dan, L.I., 2016. The road towards future Internet. Journal of communications and information networks, 1(1), pp.86-97.

Kumar, V.V., Devi, M., Raja, P.V., Kanmani, P., Priya, V., Sudhakar, S. and Sujatha, K., 2020. Design of peer-to-peer protocol with sensible and secure IoT communication for future internet architecture. Microprocessors and Microsystems, 78, p.103216.
